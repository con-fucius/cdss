HOMESTRETCH

1. Offline-first protocol execution with eventual sync
Network dropout is not an edge case in Kenya — it is the norm on many routes. 
The system must run the entire dispatch script and field protocol locally when connectivity is lost, queue every write, and sync when signal returns. 
The service worker exists but sync reliability and conflict resolution when the server and device diverge mid-incident have not been tested against real Kenyan network conditions (GSM dropouts, not WiFi blips).

2. County referral network awareness
Kenya's referral chain is Level 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6. 
An ambulance does not always go to the nearest facility — it goes to the highest level the patient's condition requires that has capacity within the county. 
The facility registry must encode facility level and county, and routing must respect the referral chain.
Routing a polytrauma patient to a Level 3 facility because it is 2km closer than the Level 5 regional hospital is a protocol failure.

3. Known hazard zones and road condition overlays
Nairobi's road network during peak hours, flood-prone routes in western Kenya, road closures after heavy rain in the highlands — none of this is in a static map.
ETA calculations based on straight-line distance or normal traffic assumptions are meaningless. 
Integration with a live road condition source (even a crowd-sourced one like Waze's API or/and a simple manually-maintained hazard layer updated by dispatchers) prevents routing ambulances into impassable roads.

4. Language and dialect switching
The NLP extractor must be trained on English and standard Swahili/sheng. 
The transcription model must support language switching mid-call, and the pre-arrival instructions an additionally be deliverable in the caller's language or both in English and Swahili. 

5. Hospital diversion and bypass alerts
Kenyatta National Hospital goes on bypass regularly. County referral hospitals run out of blood, oxygen, and theatre capacity routinely. 
The facility registry must expose a real-time diversion status field that facilities can update themselves (a simple authenticated POST endpoint), and routing must exclude diverted facilities. 
Without this the system routes ambulances to facilities that will immediately redirect them, costing 20–40 minutes.

6. Multi-casualty incident mode
Road traffic accidents involving matatus routinely produce 10–30 casualties simultaneously. 
The current incident model is one incident, one patient.
Multi-casualty mode must allow a single incident record with multiple casualty slots, individual triage scores per casualty (START triage at minimum), and a unified dispatch request that calls multiple units.
A single-patient assumption in a country where the most common emergency is a matatu accident is a structural failure.

7. Next-of-kin notification
In Kenya, a patient admitted to hospital without family notification creates immediate chaos — family members arrive hours later, demand information, interfere with treatment, and sometimes remove patients against medical advice.
An automated SMS (via Africa's Talking API, which has Kenyan carrier coverage) sent to a next-of-kin number collected at incident creation — "Your family member has been taken to General Hospital, reference number 12345, contact 0800-XXX" — reduces this significantly. Africa's Talking has a free tier and works on feature phones.

8. Controlled substance and blood product availability
A paramedic administering morphine in the field needs to know it will be restocked at the facility.
A patient needing O-negative blood in a trauma case needs to arrive at a facility that has it. 
The facility registry must include current availability of critical consumables — not a theoretical formulary but actual current stock reported by facilities. 
This is achievable with a simple facility-side update endpoint and transforms routing from "nearest appropriate facility" to "nearest appropriate facility that can actually complete care."

9. Incident pattern reporting for county health offices
KE's health teams make resource allocation decisions (where to position ambulances, road sections needing trauma readiness, facilities needing capacity investment) based on paper records and intuition, digital incident data does not reach them in a usable form.
A weekly automated PDF report — incidents by sub-county, average response times, most common presentations, busiest time windows — sent to a configured email address via a scheduled job closes the loop between operational data and health system planning. This is the feature that justifies the system's existence to county government and determines whether it gets funded and expanded.

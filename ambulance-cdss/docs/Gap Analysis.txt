Gap analysis

---

What the system is versus what it needs to be

The system has impressive structural depth: protocol runners, NLP extraction, triage ranking, facility routing, NEWS2/GCS scoring, handoff summary generation, a receiving UI, transcription endpoints, a service worker for offline operation. 
The engineering foundations are sound. The problem is that these components are not wired into a coherent, time-pressured clinical workflow. They exist as features that a developer could demonstrate in isolation. 
They do not yet form a system that a dispatcher, paramedic, or ER nurse can use under pressure without training, without thinking about the technology, and without the technology failing them at the worst moment.

---

 Gap 1 — The dispatcher's first 60 seconds are broken

The most critical moment in any emergency call is the first 60 seconds. The dispatcher is on the phone with a panicking caller. They need chief complaint, location, and priority within that window.

What exists: A form-based protocol runner that requires the dispatcher to manually type or click through structured questions one at a time. 
The NLP extractor and transcription endpoint exist on the backend but are not connected to the dispatcher's active call flow. 
The map exists but requires the dispatcher to find coordinates or click a map while simultaneously talking to a caller.

What happens in reality: The dispatcher is listening to the caller say "my husband collapsed he's not breathing please help us we're at the petrol station on Ngong Road near the junction." 
The system offers them a text field labeled "Chief Complaint" and a map they have to interact with. The transcript is either not being captured or being captured in a panel the dispatcher has to consciously check.

What is missing:
- The transcription must start automatically the moment a call is opened, not on a button press. A dispatcher under pressure will not remember to click "Start Transcription."
- The NLP extractor's output must auto-populate the chief complaint field and trigger protocol selection immediately, without the dispatcher having to read, validate, and manually transfer what the system already extracted.
- The map's location search must work by address and landmark text, not just by clicking a blank map. "Petrol station Ngong Road junction" must resolve to a pin.
- Protocol selection must be suggested automatically from the extracted chief complaint, with one confirm-tap, not a manual browse.

---

 Gap 2 — The dispatcher has no ambient awareness of the incident queue

What exists: 'GET /dashboard/active-incidents' exists on the backend. 'dashboard.html' exists in the dispatcher UI.

What is missing: When a second call comes in while the first is active, the dispatcher has no way to see both simultaneously in a useful way. 
The dashboard does not update in real time — it requires a page refresh or manual navigation. 
There is no WebSocket or SSE push to the dispatcher console. A dispatcher managing four concurrent incidents is essentially operating blind on incidents 2, 3, and 4.

More critically: there is no alert when a P1 incident has been sitting in 'dispatched' status for more than a defined number of minutes without moving to 'on_scene'. 
That silence is a system failure the dispatcher should be alerted to automatically.

---

 Gap 3 — The paramedic's interface is not survivable under field conditions

What exists: A PWA field console with a service worker, tabs for protocol steps, vitals, medications, field log, and handoff summary.

What happens in reality: A paramedic arrives on scene. They have gloves on. Their hands may be blood-contaminated. They are kneeling on the ground. They have 90 seconds to assess and begin treatment. 

The current interface requires them to:
- Navigate to the correct tab
- Find the correct protocol step
- Tap a small checkbox or button to mark it complete
- Navigate to another tab to enter vitals
- Type numeric values into individual input fields

What is missing:
- Voice command for step completion. "Step complete" or "next step" should advance the checklist without touching the screen.
- Voice entry for vitals. "BP 90 over 60" should populate the fields. The transcription module exists but is not connected to the field UI's vitals form.
- Large-target single-action screens. When a step requires a binary decision (airway clear yes/no, pulse present yes/no), the entire screen should be that decision — not a small button in a tab.
- Automatic NEWS2 calculation trigger. Right now vitals are entered and submitted separately. NEWS2 should compute and display the moment the last required field is populated, not after a separate submit action.
- The service worker's offline queue must surface its status to the user. If the device goes offline mid-incident, the paramedic must know which actions are queued locally and which have been confirmed by the server. Silence on connectivity status is dangerous.

---

 Gap 4 — The facility routing decision is not actually being made by the system

What exists: 'POST /incidents/{id}/route' calls the facility registry, returns a ranked list, and the field UI shows it.

What is missing: 
The system does not make a recommendation. It returns a list. The paramedic, under pressure, with a critical patient, is looking at three facility names and a distance figure. 
The system does not say "Route to General Hospital — nearest ALS-capable facility, 8 minutes, capacity confirmed available." 
It does not distinguish between facilities by capability match to the patient's condition. It does not account for the fact that the nearest facility may not have the right specialist or equipment for this specific presentation.

The facility routing must go from "here is a list" to "here is our recommendation and here is why, tap to confirm." The paramedic confirms, not decides.

Additionally: 
Once a facility is selected, the system must notify the receiving facility automatically and immediately. 
Right now 'report_admission' is called but there is no confirmation that the receiving facility actually received and acknowledged the notification. 
A failed notification must retry and alert the dispatcher.

---

 Gap 5 — The receiving facility interface is passive

What exists: 'receiving-ui/' renders incoming handoff summaries.

What is missing: The receiving UI is a read-only display of a document the paramedic already generated. It offers nothing to the ER team in terms of preparation or action.

What a real ER needs:
- Active alert the moment an ambulance is routed to them with an ETA countdown. Not "check this page when you feel like it" — a persistent alert that demands acknowledgement.
- The handoff summary must arrive at the receiving facility before the ambulance does, with enough lead time for the ER to prepare. Right now 'report_admission' is called at disposition, which may be correct timing — but the receiving UI has no notification mechanism. A nurse at the receiving facility would have to be watching this screen, refreshing it, to know an ambulance is 8 minutes out.
- The receiving UI must be WebSocket-driven. The moment 'POST /incidents/{id}/route' completes successfully, the receiving facility's screen must alert without any user action.
- Acknowledgement. The ER must be able to confirm receipt ("ER team notified, bay 3 being prepared") and that confirmation must be visible to both the dispatcher and the paramedic.

---

 Gap 6 — No escalation or deterioration detection during transport

What exists: Vitals are logged with timestamps. NEWS2 is computed per entry. The handoff summary shows the highest NEWS2 during transport.

What is missing: 
If a patient's NEWS2 goes from 4 to 8 between two vitals readings during transport, nothing happens. 
No alert to the paramedic. No alert to the dispatcher. No upgrade of the priority code. No automatic notification to the receiving facility that the patient has deteriorated.

This is not a nice-to-have. 
A patient who arrests in the back of an ambulance during transport, while NEWS2 was trending upward over the previous three vitals readings, is a preventable death if the trend was visible and acted on. 
The data exists in the system. The calculation exists. The alert mechanism does not.

---

 Gap 7 — Pre-arrival instructions are not actually delivered to the caller

What exists: The terminal outcome of a dispatch protocol includes 'pre_arrival_instructions' — a list of steps to give the caller (CPR instructions, bleeding control, positioning, etc.).

What is missing: 
These instructions are displayed on the dispatcher's screen. 
There is no mechanism to ensure the dispatcher is actually reading them to the caller, reading them in the right order, or that the caller is following them. 
For cardiac arrest, the 4–6 minutes before the ambulance arrives are the most critical window. The quality of the pre-arrival instruction delivery is not tracked, not timed, and not supported.

At minimum: the instructions should display one at a time with a timing indicator, with an explicit "caller acknowledged" tap between each step. This creates an audit trail and keeps the dispatcher on task.

---

 Gap 8 — No communication channel between dispatcher and paramedic within the system

What exists: The incident record is shared. Both the dispatcher console and field console read from the same incident.

What is missing: 
If a dispatcher gets additional information from the caller after the ambulance has been dispatched (caller reports patient is now conscious, caller reports a second victim, caller reports the location is actually further down the road), there is no way to push that information to the paramedic's device within the system. The dispatcher would have to use a radio or phone call outside the system, and that information would not be recorded in the incident log.

A simple dispatcher note or update mechanism that pushes to the field console via WebSocket would close this. It is not a complex feature. It is a critical operational gap.

---

 Gap 9 — The NLP extractor is not actually being evaluated for accuracy

What exists: 'nlp_extractor.py' extracts entities. 'confidence_score' is stored on incident records.

What is missing: 
There is no feedback loop. 
When the NLP extractor says the chief complaint is "chest pain" and the actual complaint was "difficulty breathing," there is no mechanism for the dispatcher to correct this in a structured way that feeds back into improving extraction quality. 
The correction goes into the incident record but does not improve the extractor.

More critically: there is no threshold below which the system refuses to auto-populate a field from a low-confidence extraction. A chief complaint extracted with 0.3 confidence that auto-selects the wrong protocol is more dangerous than no extraction at all.

---

 Gap 10 — Authentication and role separation are incomplete

What exists: 'auth.py' exists.

What is missing based on operational reality:
- A dispatcher must not be able to access the field console and vice versa without explicit role assignment.
- A receiving facility must be able to log in and see only their facility's incoming incidents.
- An admin must be able to see all incidents, all users, and the governance state of all protocols.
- Session expiry during an active incident must not log out and lose state. An authenticated dispatcher who's mid-call and whose token expires must be silently re-authenticated in the background, not kicked to a login screen.

---
Gap 11 — The protocol governance gap is still open and dangerous

The three dispatch protocol JSON files still have placeholder 'approved_by' and 'approved_date' fields. 
Per the governance enforcement added earlier in this session, all three are currently rejected at startup — meaning the system has zero active dispatch protocols. 
A dispatcher opening the system right now cannot run a single call through a structured protocol. They would see "no protocol matched" for every chief complaint.

This is not a future concern. This is the current state. Until real names and dates are in those JSON files and approved by the medical director, the entire Mode 1 dispatch protocol system is inoperative.

---

 Implementation priority order

1. WebSocket push — dispatcher console and receiving UI must receive real-time updates. This is the foundation everything else depends on. Without it, the dashboard, the receiving alert, the paramedic-to-dispatcher note, and the deterioration alert all cannot work.
2. Auto-start transcription + auto-population — transcription begins on call open, NLP output populates fields immediately, protocol is suggested with one confirmation tap. This closes the 60-second gap.
3. Map address/landmark geocoding — address search input that resolves to a pin, using a real geocoding API (Nominatim is free and works for Kenya). The current click-on-blank-map approach is unusable under pressure.
4. Deterioration alert — compare successive NEWS2 scores, alert on significant increase, push to dispatcher, upgrade priority if threshold crossed.
5. One-screen critical decisions for field — when a protocol step requires a binary yes/no, render it full-screen. No navigation, no tabs.
6. Pre-arrival instruction relay mode — one instruction at a time, timed, with caller-acknowledged tap.
7. Receiving facility WebSocket alert — alert the moment 'POST /incidents/{id}/route' confirms routing to their facility, with ETA countdown and acknowledgement mechanism.
8. Protocol governance — the three protocols need real approval data before the system can process a single call through Mode 1.
9. Confidence threshold on NLP auto-population — below a configurable threshold, flag for manual confirmation rather than auto-populate.
10. Dispatcher-to-paramedic in-system notes — simple WebSocket-pushed text updates tied to the incident record.
